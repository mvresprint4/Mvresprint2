Appendix A: Canonical Clarifications and Operational Definitions (TLBSS Reference Corpus)

A.1 Purpose and Status

This Appendix forms part of the authoritative reference corpus of the Tri-Lateral Binary System Substrate (TLBSS).
The purpose of this Appendix is to consolidate operational definitions, interpretive clarifications, and implementation-level specifications necessary for consistent application of the framework across theoretical, computational, biological, empirical, and interdisciplinary domains.
This Appendix does not modify, replace, supersede, or amend Axioms L1–L7. Rather, it establishes canonical interpretations and definitions that govern the application and implementation of the framework where ambiguity may otherwise arise.
Unless explicitly superseded by a future revision of the framework, the contents of this Appendix shall be considered normative.

A.2 Canonical Definition of Coherence (C)

Coherence (C) is the primary stability metric of the substrate and serves as the governing measure of internal structural consistency.
Within operational implementations of TLBSS, coherence is represented by a bounded stability function derived from variance across the historical register state.
The coherence metric shall satisfy the following conditions:
1. Coherence is bounded within the interval:
   0 ≤ C ≤ 1
2. Lower variance corresponds to higher coherence.
3. Higher variance corresponds to lower coherence.
4. Perfect coherence corresponds to maximal stability.
5. Coherence functions as the principal evaluative metric for admissibility, stabilization, saturation, and closure conditions.
Accordingly, coherence is not merely a philosophical abstraction but an operationally measurable and empirically testable property of the substrate.

A.3 Interpretation of State Expansion (ΔS)

Within TLBSS, the symbol ΔS denotes change in state-space expansion.
State-space expansion refers to the increase or decrease in the set of admissible system configurations available to a given frame.
The symbol ΔS shall not be interpreted as thermodynamic entropy.
For interdisciplinary publications involving physics, biology, information theory, or related disciplines, authors are encouraged to include the following notation statement:
"Within TLBSS, ΔS denotes state-space expansion and does not represent thermodynamic entropy."

A.4 Canonical Signal Definitions

The framework recognizes two primary signal representations.
A.4.1 Raw Signal
σ denotes a raw influence signal generated through cross-entity interaction and logical evaluation.
The raw signal exists prior to masking, transformation, or contextual modification.
A.4.2 Effective Signal
σ̃ denotes a transformed, masked, encrypted, or otherwise modified influence signal.
The effective signal represents the operational form of influence acting upon a target state following substrate processing.
Whenever transformation has occurred, σ̃ shall be considered the preferred canonical notation.

A.5 Resonance as a Mandatory Transitional Phase

Resonance constitutes a required phase within the canonical state-transition architecture.
No state transition shall proceed directly from threshold evaluation to commitment.
The canonical transition sequence is defined as:
Signal Generation → Threshold Evaluation → Resonance → Commitment → Audit
Within this sequence:
• Threshold evaluation determines admissibility.
• Resonance determines structural compatibility and stabilization.
• Commitment records the resulting state.
• Audit verifies coherence and boundary compliance.
Descriptions omitting resonance shall be regarded as abbreviated representations rather than complete operational specifications.

A.6 Ring-3 Embodiment Layer

Entity-C operates within a constrained execution environment designated Ring-3.
Ring-3 is a formal architectural layer of the substrate and shall not be interpreted as a metaphorical construct.
The Ring-3 layer enforces:
• latency,
• embodiment constraints,
• bounded resource accessibility,
• restricted execution privileges,
• prohibition of autonomous privilege escalation.
These limitations establish the structural conditions under which biological and embodied systems operate.
The existence of constraint is therefore a design property of the substrate rather than an implementation artifact.

A.7 Audit-Guardian

Audit-Guardian functions as the coherence certification and boundary verification mechanism of the substrate.
Audit-Guardian is responsible for:
• coherence verification,
• admissibility certification,
• boundary monitoring,
• saturation observation,
• transition validation.
Audit-Guardian possesses no autonomous agency.
Audit-Guardian generates no independent state.
Audit-Guardian performs no self-directed decision making.
Its role is exclusively evaluative and certifying.
Accordingly, Audit-Guardian shall be understood as a passive structural observer rather than an active substrate participant.

A.8 Frame Definition

A Frame is a bounded operational context within which state evaluation, coherence assessment, and admissibility determination occur.
Each frame establishes:
• its own admissible state-space,
• coherence constraints,
• evaluative boundaries,
• transition conditions.
Frames provide the structural environment in which substrate operations are interpreted and resolved.
State evolution occurs within a frame.
Frame Transition occurs beyond a frame.

A.9 Frame Transition

Frame Transition represents movement from one governing frame to a higher-order frame following the saturation or irresolvable contradiction of the originating frame.
Frame Transition is not equivalent to growth.
Growth expands an existing frame.
Frame Transition replaces the governing frame itself.
Accordingly, Frame Transition constitutes a boundary-resolution mechanism rather than a state-expansion mechanism.
This interpretation shall govern all references to Axiom L7.
